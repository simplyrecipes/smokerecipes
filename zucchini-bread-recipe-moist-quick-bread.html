A Note From My Kitchen

The biggest secret to a great loaf is managing zucchini moisture. Zucchini holds a lot of water, and that’s what makes the crumb so tender—but too much can weigh the batter down. I like to squeeze out the excess until the zucchini feels damp rather than dripping, which helps the bread bake evenly and slice cleanly.

When combining wet and dry ingredients, aim for “just combined.” Overmixing can make quick breads bake up tougher and less tender. You’re looking for a thick batter that’s cohesive, with no big pockets of flour—small streaks are okay because they’ll finish blending as you fold.

Oven timing can vary a little depending on your pan and how accurate your oven runs. If the top is browning quickly before the center is done, don’t panic—just keep baking until the toothpick test comes out clean. The loaf should look set and feel springy in the center when gently pressed.

If you want to play with flavor without changing the base recipe, focus on serving ideas instead: warm a slice slightly and enjoy it plain, or add a simple optional icing (below) for a bakery-style finish. The loaf is also lovely chilled, when the crumb feels extra dense and moist.

Ingredient Notes and Easy Swaps:

This loaf relies on everyday baking staples working together: zucchini for moisture, sugars for sweetness and tenderness, oil for a soft crumb, and a balanced lift from baking powder and baking soda. Cinnamon brings that classic warm spice note that makes zucchini bread taste like a true treat. Below are ingredient notes to help you understand the “why,” plus easy ways to adapt based on what you have—without changing the recipe’s core method. (Any swaps are optional and meant as guidance for future bakes.)

  • Grated fresh zucchini: This is the moisture-maker and the reason the loaf stays tender. Grate it finely for a more even crumb, and squeeze out excess water so the batter doesn’t turn loose or gummy.
  • All-purpose flour: Provides structure so the loaf rises and slices neatly. If your flour is clumpy, whisking it with the leaveners helps prevent dense spots and keeps the crumb consistent.
  • Granulated sugar: Gives clean sweetness and helps the loaf brown. It also supports a softer texture by holding onto moisture as the bread cools.
  • Brown sugar: Adds a deeper sweetness and a subtle caramel note. It also contributes to a slightly more moist, tender bite compared to using only granulated sugar.
  • Vegetable oil: Oil keeps quick breads soft and plush, even after refrigeration. If you’re choosing an oil, pick one with a neutral flavor so the cinnamon and zucchini shine.
  • Large eggs: Eggs bind the batter and help it rise while baking. For best results, use eggs that mix smoothly into the sugars so the batter looks glossy rather than separated.
  • Baking powder: Adds lift for a lighter crumb. Make sure it’s fresh—older baking powder can lead to a loaf that bakes up flatter and heavier.
  • Baking soda: Helps with browning and works with the batter’s moisture for a good rise. It’s powerful, so measuring accurately prevents an off taste and keeps the loaf balanced.
  • Ground cinnamon: The warm spice that makes the loaf smell like a bakery. If your cinnamon has been sitting for years, a fresher jar will give you a noticeably brighter aroma.
  • Pinch of salt: Salt sharpens the sweetness and makes the cinnamon taste more pronounced. Even a small pinch helps the loaf taste full and not flat.
Tool / Substitution Note: A standard loaf pan works best here—grease it well so the bread releases cleanly. If you don’t have a whisk, you can still combine the dry ingredients by stirring thoroughly with a fork; the goal is even distribution of the leaveners and cinnamon.

How to Make zucchini bread loaf (Step-by-Step)

Step 1: Heat your oven to 350°F (175°C) so it’s fully ready when the batter is mixed. Grease your loaf pan generously, making sure the corners are coated so the edges don’t stick.

Step 2: Grate the fresh zucchini and then squeeze out the extra moisture until it’s no longer dripping. Set it aside—you want it ready to fold in so the batter comes together quickly.

Step 3: In a mixing bowl, combine the flour with baking powder, baking soda, cinnamon, and a pinch of salt. Whisking well here helps the loaf rise evenly and prevents pockets of spice or leavener.

Step 4: In a second bowl, beat the eggs with both sugars until they look well blended and slightly glossy. Add the zucchini and vegetable oil, then mix until the wet mixture looks cohesive and evenly speckled with zucchini.

Step 5: Add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture and fold gently just until the flour disappears. Stop as soon as the batter looks combined—overmixing can make the bread less tender.

Step 6: Pour the batter into your prepared loaf pan and bake for about 60 minutes. You’ll know it’s done when a toothpick inserted into the center comes out clean and the top looks set and deeply golden.

Optional Icing:

If you’d like a simple finish, whisk powdered sugar with a small splash of milk until smooth, then stir in a pinch of ground cinnamon. Drizzle over the fully cooled loaf so it sets nicely and doesn’t melt into the crumb.

Tips For Success:

  • Squeeze the zucchini well: Damp is good, dripping is not—removing excess water helps the loaf bake through without a gummy center.
  • Fold gently: Mix only until combined to keep the crumb soft and tender.
  • Use the toothpick test: Check the center; a clean toothpick is the best signal that the loaf is baked through.
  • Cool before slicing: Letting it cool helps the crumb set so slices stay neat and moist.
  • Expect even better flavor tomorrow: Like many quick breads, this zucchini bread tastes richer after it rests.

Frequently Asked Questions:

Why do I need to squeeze the zucchini?

Zucchini carries a lot of moisture, and squeezing removes excess water that can make the loaf bake up heavy or underdone in the center. You still keep enough moisture for that tender crumb zucchini bread is known for.

How do I know when this moist quick bread is done?

Look for a set, golden top and use a toothpick in the center—if it comes out clean, it’s ready. If the toothpick shows wet batter, bake a little longer and test again.

Can I serve this as breakfast?

Yes—this moist quick bread is great with coffee or tea and holds up well for grab-and-go slices. The cinnamon and zucchini make it feel special without being complicated.
