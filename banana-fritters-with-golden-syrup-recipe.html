A Note From My Kitchen

The key to great fritters is getting the oil hot enough before the first spoonful of batter goes in. If the oil is too cool, the fritters can soak up oil and turn heavy; if it’s too hot, they’ll brown too quickly on the outside before the inside sets. Medium heat is your friend—steady, not rushed.

When you mix the batter, aim for “just combined.” That means you should stop stirring as soon as you no longer see dry flour streaks. A few small lumps are totally fine and actually help keep the fritters tender instead of bready.

As they fry, watch for the edges to look set and the surface to develop a deeper golden tone before flipping. You’ll also notice the bubbling calm down slightly once the outside starts to crisp. That’s a great visual cue that they’re ready to turn.

If you want to tweak the serving style, you can absolutely add fresh fruit on the side (as suggested) for brightness. And if you’re out of golden syrup, you can still fry the fritters and serve them warm—just choose a sweet topping you already enjoy, while keeping the recipe itself exactly as written.

Ingredient Notes and Easy Swaps:

This recipe keeps the ingredient list short, which means each item really matters. Ripe bananas bring sweetness and moisture, while flour and egg give structure so the fritters hold their shape in the oil. Baking powder adds a little lift so they don’t feel dense. Milk loosens the batter to a spoonable consistency, and the final drizzle of syrup adds shine and that classic dessert finish. Below are helpful notes and beginner-friendly swap ideas that keep the spirit of the recipe while staying practical in a real kitchen.

  • Ripe bananas: The riper they are, the sweeter and more fragrant your fritters will taste. If your bananas are just barely ripe, mash them extra well for a smoother batter and a more even texture.
  • All-purpose flour: This gives the fritters their body and helps them fry up with crisp edges. If your flour is clumpy, a quick fluff with a fork before measuring helps keep the batter from turning heavy.
  • Baking powder: This adds lightness so the fritters puff slightly instead of frying up flat. If yours has been sitting in the pantry for ages, fresher baking powder tends to give a better lift and a nicer interior.
  • Milk: Milk makes the batter spoonable and helps everything blend without overmixing. If you prefer a different milk type, choose one you already use for cooking—just aim for the same pourable consistency.
  • Egg: The egg binds the batter so the fritters hold together when you flip them. If your egg is cold from the fridge, letting it sit for a few minutes can help it mix in more smoothly.
  • Golden syrup: This is the signature finishing touch—sweet, glossy, and perfect for drizzling. Warm it briefly (just until loose) if you want a thinner drizzle that coats the fritters evenly.
  • Oil for frying: Use an oil that performs well for frying and can handle steady heat. Keep the oil depth consistent (about half an inch) so the fritters cook evenly without needing a deep fryer.
  • Fresh fruit (for serving): Serving with fruit adds a bright, fresh contrast to the warm fried exterior and sweet syrup. Keep it simple—just a small side portion so the fritters stay the star.
Tool / Substitution Note: A medium cookie scoop or a tablespoon makes portioning the batter neat and consistent, which helps the fritters cook evenly. If you don’t have one, use two spoons—one to scoop and one to nudge the batter into the oil.

How to Make banana fritters with golden syrup (Step-by-Step)

Step 1: Peel the ripe bananas and place them in a large mixing bowl. Mash until the mixture looks smooth and mostly lump-free, like a thick banana puree with a glossy, creamy look.

Step 2: Add the flour and baking powder, then pour in the milk and crack in the egg. Stir gently just until you don’t see dry flour anymore—stop early rather than mixing too much so the fritters stay tender.

Step 3: Pour oil into a frying pan so it reaches about half an inch up the side. Heat it over medium until it looks shimmering and ready—steady heat helps the fritters brown evenly without burning.

Step 4: Spoon small dollops of batter into the hot oil, leaving space between each one. Fry about 2 minutes per side, flipping when they’re deeply golden and the edges look crisp and set.

Step 5: Lift the cooked fritters out and place them on paper towels. Let them sit briefly so any extra oil drains away, keeping the outside pleasantly crisp.

Step 6: While the fritters are still warm, drizzle them generously with golden syrup. Serve right away so you get that perfect contrast of crisp exterior, soft center, and glossy sweetness.

Optional Icing:

If you’d like an extra finish (completely optional), stir powdered sugar with a small splash of milk until smooth and drizzleable, then spoon it lightly over the warm fritters. Let it set for a minute before serving for a delicate sweet sheen.

Tips For Success:

  • Don’t overmix the batter: Stir only until combined to keep the inside soft and tender rather than chewy.
  • Keep the oil at medium heat: A steady temperature helps the fritters cook through while turning evenly golden.
  • Fry in small batches: Crowding the pan can drop the oil temperature and make the fritters greasy instead of crisp.
  • Drain briefly, then drizzle: A short rest on paper towels keeps the exterior crisp before the syrup goes on.
  • Serve warm: The texture contrast is best right after frying, when the edges are crisp and the center is fluffy.

Frequently Asked Questions:

Can I make these ahead of time?

They’re best served warm right after frying for the crispiest texture. If you do make them earlier, rewarm briefly so the exterior perks back up before adding the syrup.

How do I know when the oil is ready?

Look for a gentle shimmer on the oil surface and steady heat over medium. When batter hits the oil, it should bubble actively right away without smoking or browning instantly.

Why did my fritters turn out oily?

This usually happens when the oil isn’t hot enough, so the batter absorbs oil before it sets. Keep the heat at medium and avoid overcrowding the pan so the temperature stays stable.
